A Bayesian View of Neural Networks

Part I - Bayesian Learning of Weights

There have been numerous recent papers by J.Lee et. al. (Google Brain), Matthews et. al. (Cambridge), etc. studying the behavior of wide deep neural networks in the infinite width limit. In that regime, these neural networks can be thought of as Gaussian processes, which allows for a Bayesian treatment. In this first post, I will provide a gentle introduction in this subject.

Bayes’ rule

We start with the well-loved Bayes rule: P(H∣E)=P(E∣H)P(H)P(E). P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H)P(H)}{P(E)}.P(H∣E)=P(E)P(E∣H)P(H)​. To understand this rule:

  1. HHH stands for hypothesis and EEE for evidence.
  2. P(H∣E) P(H \mid E) P(H∣E) is the posterior probability, which is the probability of HHH given that we have observed EEE
  3. As opposed to that, the prior P(H)P(H)P(H) is the prior, i.e. the probability of HHH when we have no information on EEE.
  4. P(E∣H) P(E \mid H) P(E∣H) is the likelihood of observing EEE when we are under hypothesis HHH, and
  5. P(E)P(E)P(E) is the marginal probability of evidence EEE happens regardless of hypothesis. What Bayes rule gives us is a way to update our belief on the probability distribution of the hypotheses as we gather more evidence/information.

Bayesian learning of weights

In the context of machine learning, we can think of the hypotheses as the weights w\bold ww in the neural network, and the evidence as the training data D\bold DD. In the step of initialization, we often let the weights w\bold ww to be randomly generated, usually from a Gaussian with sufficiently large variance. In the Bayesian treatment, we can think of this as our prior belief on the weights: w∼N(0,Σ) \bold w \sim \mathcal N(0, \bold \Sigma) w∼N(0,Σ). Then, using Bayes rule, for a model of neural network, we can obtain the posterior probability of the weights given that we have seen training data, i.e. we updated our belief as a consequence of having observed the data.

Comparison with MLE

In the usual approach, we use gradient descent to find

wMLE∗=arg⁡max⁡wP(D∣w),\mathbf{w^*_{MLE}}= \arg\max_{\mathbf{w}} P(D \mid \mathbf{w}),wMLE∗​=argwmax​P(D∣w),

which amounts to find a single parameter that best fits our data given the current model. On the other hand, the Bayesian approach tries to understand the distribution of parameters given the model and the training data. As a result, we can also understand the predictive distribution of the model: p(f(x)∣D,w)=∫f(x,w)P(w∣D)dw p(f(\bold x) \mid D, \bold w) = \int f(\bold x, \bold w) P(\bold w \mid D) d\bold w p(f(x)∣D,w)=∫f(x,w)P(w∣D)dw This means that we can also compute the confidence in the model output.

Another estimator that arises naturally from the Bayesian approach is the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator: wMAP∗=arg⁡max⁡wP(D∣w)P(w), \mathbf{w^*_{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{w}} P(D \mid \mathbf{w} ) P(\mathbf{w}),wMAP∗​=argwmax​P(D∣w)P(w), which is the mode of the posterior distribution. In the case where we have a compact parameter space and a uniform prior distribution, it is clear that wMAP∗=wMLE∗\mathbf{w^*_{MAP}} = \mathbf{w^*_{MLE}}wMAP∗​=wMLE∗​. Thus, one can view the MLE as a special case of the MAP estimator. Notice that when we use a Gaussian prior with mean 000, the MAP estimator has smaller norm than the MLE. This is because large norm is punished by P(w)∝exp⁡(−12wTΣ−1w).P(\bold w) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} \bold w^T \Sigma^{-1} \bold w \right).P(w)∝exp(−21​wTΣ−1w). In the case where the data is also normally distributed with respect to the weights w\bold ww, using the MAP estimator amounts to a well-known form of regularization called ridge-regression.
