A Bayesian View of Neural Networks

Part II - Bayesian Learning of Functions

In the last part, we went over an introduction to the Bayesian approach in machine learning, where we think of a neural network model from a weight-space point of view. In this post, I will go over an alternative approach, whereby we apply Bayesian inference directly in the function space. That is, instead of thinking of the model as specifying the probability distribution over the weights in a neural network, we are thinking of it as specifying a probability distribution over functions whose domain is the space of inputs of the network, and the codomain as the space of its outputs.

Stochastic processes

A stochastic process is a collection of random variables {Xt}t∈T\{X_t\}_{t \in T}{Xt​}t∈T​ that are indexed by some set TTT. A stochastic process is a Gaussian process if for every finite collection of indices (t1,…,tn)(t_1,\dots,t_n)(t1​,…,tn​), we have that (Xt1,…,Xtn)(X_{t_1},\dots, X_{t_n})(Xt1​​,…,Xtn​​) is a multivariate Gaussian random variable.

Just like Gaussian random variables, a Gaussian process can be completely characterized by its mean and covariance functions:

m(t)=E[Xt]k(t,t′)=E[(Xt−m(t))(Xt′−m(t′))T]\begin{aligned} m(t) &= \mathbb E[X_t] \\ k(t,t') &= \mathbb E[(X_t - m(t))(X_{t'} - m(t'))^T] \end{aligned}m(t)k(t,t′)​=E[Xt​]=E[(Xt​−m(t))(Xt′​−m(t′))T]​

Usually, the index set is denoted as TTT for its interpretation as time. For example, the well-known Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process is a Gaussian process, and Brownian motion is a stochastic process that is the integral of a white noise generalized Gaussian process. These are beyond the scope of this blog post, but it could be of interest to some readers.

Bayesian inference with Gaussian processes

Just as before, we are interested in updating our belief once we have gathered more observations. That is, we want to compute the posterior distribution over functions given the training data {(xi,fi):1≤i≤n}\{(\mathbf{x}_i, f_i): 1 \leq i \leq n \}{(xi​,fi​):1≤i≤n}. For now, assume that our observations are noise-free. Suppose further that we want to obtain outputs for mmm test points {(xi∗,f∗):1≤i≤m}\{(x_i^*, f^*): 1 \leq i \leq m\}{(xi∗​,f∗):1≤i≤m}.

Let X=(x1,…,xn)X = (x_1,\dots,x_n)X=(x1​,…,xn​),, ,X∗=(x1∗,…,xm∗)X^* = (x_1^*,\dots, x_m^*)X∗=(x1∗​,…,xm∗​), f=(f1,…,fn)\mathbf{f} = (f_1,\dots, f_n)f=(f1​,…,fn​), and f∗=(f1∗,…,fm∗)\mathbf{f^*} = (f_1^*,\dots, f_m^*)f∗=(f1∗​,…,fm∗​). Because f(⋅)∼GP(0,k(⋅,⋅))f(\cdot) \sim \mathcal{GP}(0, k(\cdot, \cdot))f(⋅)∼GP(0,k(⋅,⋅)), we have that the joint distribution of these data points are

[ff∗]∼N(0,[K(X,X)K(X,X∗)K(X∗,X)K(X∗,X∗)]), \left[\begin{matrix} \mathbf{f} \\ \mathbf{f^*} \end{matrix}\right]\sim \mathcal{N} \left(0, \left[ \begin{matrix} K(X,X) & K(X,X^*) \\ K(X^*, X) & K(X^*,X^*) \end{matrix}\right] \right),[ff∗​]∼N(0,[K(X,X)K(X∗,X)​K(X,X∗)K(X∗,X∗)​]),

where K(A,B)=[k(a,b)]a∈A,b∈BK(A,B)= [k(a,b)]_{a \in A, b \in B}K(A,B)=[k(a,b)]a∈A,b∈B​ is an ∣A∣×∣B∣\left|A \right| \times \left|B \right|∣A∣×∣B∣ matrix.

To compute the posterior, we thus simply use the rule for conditioning Gaussian to obtain

f∗∣X∗,X,f∼N(μ∗,k∗),\mathbf{f^*} \mid X^*, X, \mathbf{f} \sim \mathcal N(\mu^*,k^*),f∗∣X∗,X,f∼N(μ∗,k∗),

where

μ∗=K(X∗,X)K(X,X)−1fk∗=K(X∗,X∗)−K(X∗,X)K(X,X)−1K(X,X∗).\begin{aligned} \mu^* &= K(X^*,X) K(X,X)^{-1} \mathbf{f} \\ k^* &= K(X^*,X^*) - K(X^*,X)K(X,X)^{-1} K(X,X^*) \end{aligned}.μ∗k∗​=K(X∗,X)K(X,X)−1f=K(X∗,X∗)−K(X∗,X)K(X,X)−1K(X,X∗)​.

In other words, our posterior is again a Gaussian process, with mean and covariance function given by specifying the set X∗X^*X∗ in the above formulas for μ∗\mu^*μ∗ and k∗k^*k∗.

Simulated example

Note that for the above example of the simple kernel-based neural network, for a set X={x1,…,xn}X = \{\mathbf{x_1},\dots,\mathbf{x_n}\}X={x1​,…,xn​} of nnn different inputs, if n>dn>dn>d, where ddd is the dimension of the input, then although f(x1),…,f(xn)f(\mathbf{x_1}),\dots, f(\mathbf{x_n})f(x1​),…,f(xn​) is still distributed as a multivariate Gaussian, its covariance matrix is singular, and conditioning on knowing any f(xi1),…,f(xid)f(\mathbf{x_{i_1}}), \dots, f(\mathbf{x_{i_d}})f(xi1​​),…,f(xid​​), we know what f(⋅)f(\cdot)f(⋅) is. This means that one-dimensional problems are boring because knowing value of 1 non-degenerate point results in knowing the function. (only true in the case where our observations are not noisy).

As such, let us look at a more interesting example (not arising from kernel-based neural network), where the covariance function is given by:

k(x,x′)=exp⁡(−12∥x−x′∥2).k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x'}) = \exp\left( - \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x'} \|^2 \right).k(x,x′)=exp(−21​∥x−x′∥2).

Here is a plot showing a simulation when x∈R\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb Rx∈R and the Gaussian process is defined with the above exponential decay kernel. On the left, we have sample of functions drawn from the GP prior, while on the right, we have the posterior given the five data points (blue dots). The shaded area corresponds to 2 standard deviations away from the mean of the distribution.

As opposed to the prior where the standard deviation is the same across all input points, after observing the training data, we updated our belief to have much more confidence in the area of inputs between any two observed data points. In this particular case, because the observation is noise-free, there is no uncertainty regarding the outputs at the input points where we have training data. Furthermore, as we use the exponential prior k(x,x′)=exp⁡(−12∥x−x′∥2).k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x'}) = \exp\left( - \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x'} \|^2 \right).k(x,x′)=exp(−21​∥x−x′∥2)., points that are closed together have high covariance, and so the functions drawn are smooth. As a result for the posterior, we can see that between any two given data points with high slope change (for e.g. the second and third one), we have more confidence in the prediction as the variance is smaller, as is evidenced from the smaller width of the shaded area.
